In a world as dangerous as your typical dungeon fantasy setting, demonstrations
of friendship and respect are vitally important! Groups are always stronger than
individuals, therefore strenghtening a group's bonds is the social equivalent of
leveling up. Let's take a look at the social customs a few different but related
species developed for this.

We'll look at three different modes of social interaction within each people:

- Baseline demonstrations of **friendship and respect** tend to be fairly
  informal, with few formal rules layered around the core practice. They still
  do take some effort and intentionality to perform, of course.

- **Institutional Diplomacy** is based on the same principles, but has a lot of
  formal rules layered on top. This is what representatives of different
  communities or institutions do to strenghten the bonds between the groups they
  represent.

- **Romantic coursthip** has a lot of extra rules and rituals that differentiate
  it from a platonic gesture. Wedding ceremonies are a further extension of
  this. _Diplomatic_ weddings, when they're a thing, are the ultimate
  culmination of all of all three modes, and tend to be extremely elaborate and
  extremely complex to arrange, as they might need to combine the traditions of
  different peoples.


To lend an air of authentic scholarship, we also present the most popular
scholarly theories about why a given people behaves like that. Obsiously, the
scholars are from an entirely different species. It wouldn't be scholarly to
hear them talk about themselves.


## Humans

Humans share meals. Merfolk scholars believe that this is because ancient humans
were hunter-gatherers in an environment where food could be scarce, and so
sharing what little food you had with someone else was became a sign of
affection.

- **Friendship**: The very act of "breaking bread" with someone is already seen
  as a sign of friendship and respect among humans, so humans who wish to
  demonstrate this will invite the other party to a casual meal or otherwise
  make an effort to eat together. Such meals are seen as a time to gather, talk,
  and bond. There are some basic rules of hospitality and etiquette here, but
  they are usually kept simple when compared to the other two modes of
  interaction below. Repeatedly refusing such invitations can be seen as
  anti-social behavior. This is baffling to dragonborn, who consider meals to be
  private affairs and therefore have no concept of table manners.

- **Romance**: There are many secondary cultural signifiers meant to
  differentiate a casual frieldy meal from a "romantic dinner", and great care
  is usually taken to avoid sending the wrong message. Wedding ceremonies
  involve big feasts with elaborate associated traditions.

- **Diplomacy and Politics**: Human diplomacy is usually conducted over a fancy
  meal provided by the party hosting the meeting. The rules of etiquette
  surrounding these meals are many and varied, and the way in which each party
  follows them or fails to do so is understood to be laden with subtext.


## Merfolk

Merfolk show appreciation for another person by wearing something that person
likes. Fairy scholars theorize that this is because merfolk are always
surrounded by very pretty things under the sea, but that the pretty is often
also a sign of danger. Wearing a beloved symbol is the opposite of a danger
sign.

- **Friendship** means objects or symbols dear to the friend. The more specific
  to the friend the symbol is, the greater the level of friendship, because it
  shows the merfolk made the effort to find this out. Different merfolk cultures
  have different traditions here. For some it's the friend's favorite flower,
  for others their favorite animal or food, and others still have a custom of
  designing personal heraldic crests. Merfolk who don't know much about the
  other person but still want to show respect and a friendly attitude will
  usually reach for more generic symbols, like a family crest or flag.

- **Romance**: Merfolk cultures have a specific kind jewelry that symbolizes
  courtship. Wearing a token of this type with symbols enjoyed by the desired
  individual in public is an extremely obvious declaration of love. Specificity
  is very important in this case. Note that the token is not a gift - the
  merfolk is expected to discover the appropriate symbol and wear it themselves.
  Merfolk weddings are visually spectacular, and a common tradition is for the
  couple to alter their personal crest to include an element of the spouse's
  crest.

- **Diplomacy and Politics**: Merfolk diplomats care more about their choice of
  wardrobe than about the specific side activities that might happen during
  negotiations. More generic symbols are preferred here as diplomats are there
  as representatives of their respective employers, not as individuals. A
  meeting between two merfolk diplomats will have each of them wearing livery in
  the _other party's_ colors instead of their own, which is baffling to
  humans.

## Fairies

Fairies (i.e, pixies and other related small nature spirits) show appreciation
by repeatedly declaring it out loud, and trying to spend lots of time with the
other person, or otherwise trying to be a memorable presence in that person's
life. Dragonborn scholars theorize that their eternally childish minds and
immortal life spans might make them forget people over the years and decades if
interaction is sporadic, so these constant declarations serve as both reminders
of affection and a way of saying "please remember me".

- **Friendship** with a fairy means having a near-constant fairy companion who
  will call you "friend" and try to provide helpful hints or other small
  favors. To some, the words "hey, listen!" bring cherished memories. For
  others, they bring dread. The distinction is lost on the fairies themselves.

- **Romance** is pretty similar to friendship, but the fairy calls the other
  person "my beloved", or invents cute nicknames for them. They will often try
  to give their beloved small shiny gifts that _the fairy_ thinks are
  neat. Fairy weddings are rare, and usually imitate the most "storybook"
  version of a nearby other culture's customs.

- **Diplomacy** with fairies is completely devoid of subtext, which is baffling
  to merfolk. They say what they mean, and expect the other party to do the
  same. Promises and agreements are verbal but utterly binding - there is
  specialized magic to ensure both parties always remember them. Fairies chosen
  for diplomat roles are more serious than average and understand the importance
  of their jobs, but they still have no patience for subterfuge and indirect
  language. They also want to conclude negotiations as quickly as possible,
  because this is _work_ and work is no fun.

## Dragonborn

The reptilian dragonborn place high value on honorable behavior and martial
ability. Their definition of honor looks a lot like a knight's chivalric ideals,
but applies to all individuals, not just to a specific social class. Their shows
of appreciation are signs of respect for the other person's honor and/or
ability. Human scholars theorize that this is because dragonborn come from
highly dangerous environments where the ability to defend oneself was even more
important than the ability to find food.

- **Friendship** is demonstrated through gifts of armor, weapons, or other
  practical, survival-oriented tools. Another way to demonstrate friendship is
  through sincere promises of aid, particularly in combat. There's some
  etiquette around that meant to signal that the promise is because the
  dragonborn respects the other person's honor, not because the dragonborn doubts
  their ability. Gifted equipment doesn't have to be fancy, but it has to be
  useful.

- **Romance** also involves gifts and pledges, though there are more rules and
  traditions layered on top. Romantic gifts _are_ expected to be fancier, and to
  include a set of traditional decorations that signify courtship. A verbal
  declaration of love is a pledge of service with specific wording derived from
  dragonborn poetic traditions. Dragonborn weddings are a more elaborate version
  of this, with oaths of life-long loyalty exchanged by both sides.

- Dragonborn **diplomacy** happens on the battlefield. While actual fighting is
  usually not _desired_ or even _likely_, both parties are expected to send some
  kind of fighting force along with their diplomats and negotiators, as
  tradition says that a fight might break out at any moment. Conversely,
  negotiation is expected to include a lot of posturing and threats despite
  neither side intending to go through with them. The fighting forces should be
  as close to equivalent as possible, which usually means each side will detail
  their own forces in preliminary correspondence. Sending a _superior_ force is
  a major breach of etiquette because it signals you actually want a fight
  instead of a negotiation. All of this baffles fairies, who take the posturing
  literally.
