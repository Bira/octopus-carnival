I obviously don't need to introduce Scooby Doo to anyone who is reading
this. Everyone knows it's a show about a group of teenagers and their talking
dog friend driving around in a psychedelic van and solving mysteries. The
mysteries always involve a monster, and one of the setting's ironclad rules is
that _the monster is always fake_. It's always some crook (or a gang of them)
who decided to dress up in a scary costume to keep the civilians away while they
enact some sort of criminal scheme. Despite their personality quirks, our heroes
always manage to capture and unmask the villains through a combination of
investigative skills, careful planning, and a good dose of luck.

I've seen people describe the show as a timeless example of science and
rationality triumphing against superstition due to the above setup. While I like
the franchise a lot and I think it's awesome, I'm not sure I agree with this
particular assessment. Yeah, the monster is always fake, but this is still a
deeply weird setting with a bunch of factors that argue against that stalwart
rationalist stance.

## The Big One

First of all is, of course, the fact that the dog does talk. This is actually
_not_ an example of weirdness because unlike the other details we talk about
below, it remains just a cartoon trope. No one ever comments that it's strange
that dogs can talk - they just accept it and move on with the story. So will we.

## Fashion Crimes

The second is how often those crooks decide to dress up as monsters. This one
also started out as a genre convention. In a show about amateur detectives, it's
accepted that they'll keep running into the crimes they like to investigate no
matter where they are. However, at least one show in this franchise ("Mystery
Inc.") decided to throw a lampshade on this one and treat it as an in-setting
thing, so now it's fair game.

So yeah: a crook dressing up as a monster once is novel. If it happened twice,
it would be odd. A setting where it happens dozens of times is not a rational
place.

## Ultra-Tech Threats

The original show mostly featured monsters that were just people in
costume. Their only "real" power was looking scary, and any other fantastical
ability displayed was in the end revealed to be a clever special effect. The
flying ghost used wires; the giant dragon was a projection; the crab-man was
made of rubber. This is a big factor in the "stalwart rationalist" idea, but it
doesn't stay true.

It didn't matter very much that the monster didn't have real powers when it was
still a burly thug inside the costume and there's a "no violence allowed"
writing rule. But "no violence allowed" became "Daphne knows kung fu" after the
live action movie, and there were even a couple of other joke videos that
recognized the protagonists had a pretty good chance against a classic costumed
crook if they decided to fight. It'd be five against one, with one of the five
being a sapient Great Dane.

Because of this, and to add variety, more recent shows like "What's New Scooby
Doo" and "Mystery Inc." decided to up the stakes a bit. The monsters were still
fake, but their abilities matched their cover stories a lot more closely. The
flying ghost could really fly; the giant dragon went on real fire-spitting
rampages; the crab-man was bulletproof and could cut through steel.

These weren't just costumes any more - they were giant robots and suits of
powered armor. I think there might even be an episode or two where the
characters themselves recognized the thing the villains were trying to steal was
less valuable than the machine they were using to steal it. This gives us a
world that resembles the real one even less, especially when combined with the
frequency of costumed crime. It's a lot closer to a supers or mad science
setting than it is to our world.

And let's talk a bit about the monsters themselves, shall we? Sure, the early
ones were all just people in costumes. Whatever supernatural powers they had
were just harmless special effects. You might wonder if it would be possible for
our protagonists to stand and fight rather than run away. That actually happens
in some places! There's a Cartoon Network gag reel that has them fighting the
culprit, and the live action movies cast Sarah Michelle Gellar as Daphne and had
her fighting off hordes of mooks with her bare hands.

"What's New, Scooby Doo?" and "Mystery Inc." tried to get back to the original
series' "non-violent" roots, but the kung fu cat was already out of the bag. So,
both to take fisticuffs off the table and to have a little more variety, they
routinely had monsters whose actual capabilities matched their cover stories
very closely. The flying ghost actually flies; the dragon actually spits fire
and is strong enough to break down walls; the crab-man can cut you in half if he
gets his pincers on you.

All of these were still fake, but now the crooks were building and piloting
giant mecha and suits of powered armor instead of just wearing Halloween
costumes. So our bizarre world of dress-up crime is now also an ultra-tech
setting, since every culprit has an advanced engineering degree.
