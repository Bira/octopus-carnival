Guess what I've been doing instead of playing Hell's Rebels? And I've been
thinking about this game enough that I wanted to get out a one-post adaptation
just like I did for Skyrim a while back.

There are some spoilers below, so beware.

# Part 1: High Concept

Plot-wise, **Mouse: P. I.**  is a gritty and violent noir story. It has all the
tropes and themes of classic noir and it's also more socially aware, focusing on
themes like racism and systemic oppression with a somewhat more modern view than
the one you find in period works. We're in a city full of crime and
inequality. The bad guys are rotten, and the good guys morally grey. Everything
is at least a little dirty, but sometimes you can spot a flower blooming from
cracks in the grimy sidewalks.

The kicker here is that instead of presenting this in the stylish live-action
chiaroscuro cinematography of noir cinema, it does so with the _other_ prominent
film stile of the 1930s: black and white cartoons with "rubber hose" style
animation.

This game doesn't actually contain any Disney characters, but even so it
couldn't have been released before Steamboat Willy finally came into the public
domain. Thost rotten bad guys and dubiously good guys are all some kind of
humanoid rodent. And the flowers blooming on the sidewalk do happy little
dances.

Thirdly, the game is also "pulpy" in the sense that its plot often veers into
the fantastical, and of course there's _plenty_ of high-intensity fighting to be
had here. But noir's disillusionment with society still takes priority over the
more naive outlook of pulps.

While combat is very violent, it's not "gritty" at all. This is a movement
shooter[^1]. A Doom clone at that, though _which_ Doom it's cloning changes as
we advance through the levels and acquire more weapons and abilities. We start
pretty close to early Doom (1993), and end up closer to Doom Eternal by the end.

A tabletop campaign in this setting plays much like a classic noir game in any
other. PC origins are pretty much the same: private detectives, criminals, cops,
maybe even a mix of those. Their morals probably shouldn't be squeaky-clean, but
just like in my [Cyberpunk adaptation from a while back][1] I think it's
important to keep a core of decency to the characters that enable them to work
together and impels them to oppose those truly rotten villains. Maybe they can't
fix everything that's wrong with the world at once, but they can make things a
little better here and there.

A tabletop adaptation is not going to be nearly as kinetic as the game, so the
noir mood will take over even more. Still, here we are presented with the
perfect opportunity to include all those unrealistic FPS mechanics in our
tabletop campaign without breaking immersion. Cartoon physics covers many sins.

# Part 2: Time and Place

We're in a fictional world that follows cartoon physics and where all people are
some kind of sapient rodent. It's not an "Earth" because the geography seems to
be different and all the names definitely are, but we're still in a time and
whose vibes are a match for early 1930s US. In the game you play as Jack Pepper,
a gravelly-voiced private detective who lives in the crime-ridden city of
Mouseburg. Over the course of its levels, you take on several cases that pit you
against mobsters, crooked cops, cultists, mad scientists and, ultimately Nazis,
who turn out to be behind everything.

The game is named "Mouse", the city is named Mouseburg, but I said the
inhabitants are "rodents". This is because racial tensions are core to the
game's plot. There are three ethnicities here: tall and thin _mice_, big and
bulky _rats_, and tiny _shrews_ who are about half as tall as a mouse. Shrews
are a persecuted minority, discriminated because of their size. They've
suffered, and still suffer, indignities drawn from the plight of several
different real world people. For example, they used to have a prosperous,
thriving town that was destroyed by racists who probably wore white hoods, and
they are currently being victimized en masse by nazis. The old newspapers you
find that talk about this are very obviously slanted in their coverage.

While the analogues are clear, a lot of names are changed, usually to keep the
incongruous cartoon feel going. Not just place names or historical events (WWI
is the "Quite Big Affair"), but also elements you would expect to find in a
gritty and violent noir history get renamed. Characters don't have "blood": they
have _ink_. The word "cheese" does a lot of heavy lifting here as well. People
chew on cheese-sticks instead of smoking, they drink fondue instead of booze,
and the story reaches for blue cheese whenever it needs to mention the sort of
illegal drug that will fuck you up and get you arrested for possession. The
nazis here belong to the "Big Mouse Party" and wear lightning insignias. Most
swear words are the same, though.

While those mouse nazis are responsible for the latest crisis you're addressing
in the game, there are also plenty of hints that the persistent systemic
inequalities that allowed that crisis to happen in the first place are the fault
of wealthy industrialists and other elites. This is back when being a "commie"
was illegal, and it looks like we're still a little ways before the mouse
equivalent of the New Deal would alleviate some of the problems with this
society.

# Part 3: Characters and Their Stuff

If we're going to be faithful to the game we need to use GURPS Action with
250-point characters. A mixed-origin team probably fits the best, as a detective
agency big enough to have a full party of PIs doesn't seem to fit the cliches. A
single eternally down on his luck PI with some street criminal, reporter, and
less-crooked-than-usual cop buddies seems like a better choice.

We're in the pre-WWII 20th century here, so our Tech Level is 6. The _Pulp
Action!_ article from **Pyramid #3/8: Cliffhangers** will be invaluable here as
it has a handly list of adjustments to adapt the base Action templates for our
setting, and also includes the new Femme Fatale and Gadgeteer templates.

A game that's more faithful to the noir cliches the game is inspired by can
probably get by with lower point totals and less pulpy concepts, but I'll leave
that as an exercise to the reader.

That leaves us to handle the _cartoon_ aspects of the setting, and it looks like
I can't cheat my way out of dealing with those.

## Species Templates

**Mouse PI** leans heavily into the "Funny Animal" cartoon trope. They're mice,
rats, and shrews. This isn't exactly the same as the set of tropes that make a
game "furry". While the game uses the fact that the characters are rodents as
occasional joke fodder (such as Jack using his tail to pick locks, or the
constant mentions of cheese as a placeholder for a variety of vices), it's not
really _obsessed_ with it in the same way, say, Zootopia is.

Correspondingly, we can take a fairly light touch with our species templates,
corresponding to the three main ethnicities present in the game's story. All of
the three appear as enemies throughout the levels, and as civilians outside of
combat zones.

While the real world animals that bear these names are all different species, in
this setting they work more like ethnicities.

### Mouse [0 points]

The majority of characters in the game, both civilians and enemies, are
mice. Mice are tall and thin, more Mortimer than Mickey. They have no
adjustments. Build them as if you were building a human.

### Rat [20 points]

Rats are about as tall as mice but much bulkier, with a "square" build
reminiscent of Disney's Big Bad Pete. As enemies they tend to "tanky bruiser"
roles, but they seem to occupy more or less the same roles as mice in civilian
life. I could make them SM +1 on account of their boxy build but I don't want to
bother with that. Therefore:

Rats have +2 ST {20}. Their clothes and armor are not interchangeable with those of
mice.

### Shrew [0 points]

Shrews are to mice as halflings are to humans. This actually makes them about as
tall as Mickey Mouse himself. They don't look like Mickey, though, and have long
snouts full of sharp little teeth. As enemies, they're mostly very fast but
relatively fragile, though some notable exceptions appear in the late game. As
civilians, they're an oppressed minority and the main victims of the various
villainous plots here. Discrimination here is based on height.

Like Dungeon Fantasy halflings, Shrews are SM -2. They have ST -3 {-30}, DX+2
{40}, and Social Stigma (Minority Group) {-10}.
