I think this started in Neuromancer, like a lot of things in cyberpunk
RPGs. There, it was a very specific trait of the setting that cyberdecks were
invented by the military not that long ago, and that some of the best
ice-breaker software was still a military secret.

There's a whole sidequest in there about the protagonists getting a
near-mythical "Chinese military icebreaker" program so they can use it to
complete their main goal, and the thing is so good at its job all Case has to do
is to push a button and watch it work for the rest of the book. He still has a
part to play in the story, but it's no longer related to his hacking skills.

Shadowrun, it seems, took this element that was specific to this one thing in
the book's setting and turned into a sort of universal trope. There were at
least two supplements in the game's long history that had equipment catalogs
with "milspec gear" in them. A lot of this gear was just like something you
could find in the core book, but had better stats because it was
"military". "Military armor" was better than the best core book armor, "battle
rifles" were better than assault rifles, "military computers" had stats that
went way past the limits established in the core, "anti-tank missiles" were
better than mere anti-vehicle ones, there were even "military blades" that did
more damage.

I first read these books when I was fairly young, and it took me a while to
learn that this trope doesn't actually make much sense.

## So, What Makes Sense?

I mean, sure, there are things that are simply out of reach of law-abiding
civilians. You can't walk into a dealership and buy a fully armed tank. But PCs
in these games have access to a huge gear list right at character creation, and
that gear list is _already_ largely composed of equipment like assault rifles,
body armor, and encrypted radios.

In these settings, I would expect "military grade" to be at around the middle of
the equipment curve or slightly below that. A single stylish urban mercenary is
free to spend their ill-gotten gains on a customized rifle full of accessories
and loaded with special ammunition in exotic calibers. The military has to buy
rifles by the millions, so it goes for the cheapest model that can do the job,
gives it no accessories, and buys only bog-standard ammo.

The same goes for hacking equipment, especially in the settings I prefer where
that costs about the same as real-life computers. The PC hacker can assemble a
monster rig from consumer-grade hardware. Soldier Boy carries something that
might be a bit more rugged but is way behind the state of the art because it's
been years since someone was able to finalize a spec and get it deployed across
the whole force. It's used for tactical support, not for hacking. Network
security will be good but less advanced than, say, that of a typical megacorp
(which counts as a civilian org).

As for hacking software, well, it would be realistic to say that the best
programs come from civilian intelligence agencies. Given recent news, it would
also be realistic to say the programs PC hackers use are leaked versions of
those same tools. Maybe even _improved_ versions, since PC hackers are often
cinematic geniuses.

Armor is generally a wash. In these games it's usually quite effective and
cheap, and can look like any sort of clothing. PCs have armored streetwear,
soldiers have armored uniforms, they protect the same amount.

How about cyberware? That's easy. I think it's safe to say there is absolutely
no such thing as a standard-issue military implant. They're just too expensive
to buy at scale. No military is going to spend that kind of money on its
rank-and-file infantry when it could use it to buy more of that big hardware
that's out of reach for individuals.
