Brancalonia is a setting for D&D 5th Edition from Italian publisher Acheron
Books, with help from other groups named Epic Party and Ignoranza Eroica. It was
published in 2021 in both English and Italian after a successful Kickstarter
campaign. This does mean it's based on the 2014 version of the rules - the 2024
one wasn't out yet!

In this post, I'll try to review the English version of the core book, also
called **Brancalonia**, which has the subtitle "Spaghetti Fantasy" in an attempt
to try to explain what it's about to an American audience.

## The End At the Beginning

Despite the organizational and translation issues I found here, **Brancalonia**
is a book near and dear to my heart. The art is _fantastic_, the jokes are
hilarious (when they land correctly), many of the individual setting elements
are pretty awesome, and the overall tone of the setting is a very welcome
departure from the high-intensity violent dungeon fantasy that seems to be the
default for D&D 5th Edition (and a lot of other D&D, come to think of it).

As of this writing, it's probably my favorite D&D setting, taking the lead from
Gaming Ballistic's Etera presented in [**Dragon Heresy**][1].

## On The Book Itself

The English PDF of **Brancalonia** I have in front of me has 198 pages. The
physical edition of the book is a 196-page hardcover.

This is a setting supplement that assumes you have access to the D&D 5th edition
core books. It was written with the 2014 rules in mind (since the 2024 ones
weren't out yet), but I imagine it wouldn't be hard to convert. Due to some of
the setting modifiers we'll discuss below, it contains rules material for PC
levels 1 to 6.

The layout is somewhat similar to official D&D books, with that light-brown
background and a workable serif font for the text and most of the
headers. Chapter and some major section headers use this Olde Timey Gothic Font
that can get hard to read at times.

The writing is generally understandable, but also obviously translated from
Italian. This means you'll find some weird sentence structures here and there,
and some of the translated names for things might not roll off the tongue as
readily. I only know Italian through its similarity to Portuguese and from some
idioms that made the transition from immigrant communities in the region of
Brazil where I live, but even I could notice some of the jokes didn't land right
because they slammed face-first into the language barrier.

There's a table of contents at the start and an index at the end, both of which
are useful. The text itself is a little less organized, as the book tries to
present its information in the "traditional" order for D&D, but struggles with
this a bit. It's common for the character creation options at the start of the
book to reference setting information on the back half, for example, or for
setting-specific rules to be spread through two or more places. There are also a
few mentions of material that ended up being cut from the core book for space,
and ended up in the first supplement, **The Macaronicon**. Thankfully they're
not very frequent and do not get in the way of actually using the material that
_is_ in this book.

## Sources of Inspiration

The world of **Brancalonia** is inspired by Italian folklore and on a particular
kind of Italian fantasy fiction. As mentioned above, the cover calls this
"spaghetti fantasy" in a reference to Spaghetti Western movies, but from what I
understand this isn't quite the right vibe. The name they use in Italian is
_Zappa i Spada_ ("Hoe and Sword"). I think this is a play on "cappa i spada",
("cape and sword") which is another name for swashbuckling fiction. So it's
kinda like swashbuckling, but less fancy.

The bedrock on which this setting is built is the Italian movie _L'Armata
Brancaleone_, known as _Brancaleone's Army_ or _For Love and Gold_ in
English. Even the name of the setting hits at this. It's about adventures in a
medieval setting that, but instead of starring noble knights and mighty wizards,
it centers on bands of peniless scallywags trying to stay alive, have some fun,
and maybe strike it rich with that one big job.

In other words, you don't play noble knights or mighty wizards, you play the
people that get hired to solve actual problems because those bigwigs are too
busy waging war and intrigue against each other. Your clients might be
villagers, merchants, nobles and warlords. The jobs might just be slightly
shifty on a good day, and involve all sorts of monsters, demons, and curses on a
bad one.

Even though you might be hired by some fancy noble or warlord, the stage here
mostly belongs to the peasants, laborers, artisans and artists that inhabit the
fields and towns of the realm. Bigwigs tend to be more distant figures when they
appear at all.

Youtube provides us with [a clip][2] of a typical party getting ready to start
their next scheme/adventure. Even if you, like me, can't understand Italian,
you'll get a pretty good idea of the look and feel that the book builds on. The
guy on the horse is Brancaleone himself, and the guy on Brancalonia's cover is
clearly based on him. Can't get more typical than that.

Other sources called out as being particularly important are Basile's _Tale of
Tales_; pretty much anything Italo Calvino ever wrote; the _Corto Maltese_
comics (despite those being set in modern days); the original tale of Pinocchio;
and a few movies American audiences might be more familiar with, like
_Ladyhawke_ and _The Princess Bride_. I'd also add _O Auto da Compadecida_
(titled _A Dog's Will_ in English). Despite being a story about 20th century
Northeastern Brazil, it's written in the same style as the kind of baroque play
that was the style in Europe in the times Brancalonia takes inspiration from,
and most of its characters would fit right in as PCs and NPCs.

## On the Intended Tone

This information is presented on the GM section at the back half of the book,
but I think it's important to know before going through the rest.

PCs are called Knaves instead of "adventurers", joining up to form a Band, which
in turn probably belongs to a larger Company. A Company might be anything from
actual mercenaries to a troupe of actors to a group of wandering priests trying
to do good. The smallest ones have a single Band, the biggest could have
dozens. The Company is the source of the Band's jobs, and its Den the place
where they return to rest after each one.

The book intends for Knaves to be "rogues with hearts of gold", and for
adventures to have something of a light-hearted and humorous tone even if they
also feature suffering and danger. The pace is slower than that of "default
D&D", and quick wits, trickery and sometimes even honest diplomacy are expected
to carry the day as or _more_ often than direct combat.

You're not conquering some "wild frontier" by inflicting high-intensity violence
eight times a day, you're wandering through a place that has been thoroughly
settled for thousands of years, interacting with its people, and dealing with
its weirdness. Fighting will sometimes be unavoidable, and we do in fact have
rules for bar brawls that might see frequent use, but actual murderhobo behavior
is frowned upon both by the book and by the people in the setting.

The setting is also "low-magic" in a very specific way: neither the PCs nor most
other people in the setting have access to the sort of reality-bending magic
that characterizes high-level D&D, but magic is still _everywhere_. It's not a
secret, and it's not seen as inherently bad or evil.

## On the Setting

Welcome to the Kingdom of Brancalonia, a boot-shaped peninsula attached to the
continent of Occasia, which is a fantasy version of Europe. You know you're not
on Earth because this is the _left_ boot.

Brancalonia has a history that's very similar to that of the historical Italian
Peninsula, with some names swapped out and flipped turnways. It was the cradle
of the great Draconian Empire, which conquered the whole world except for this
one pesky pagan village over in northern Frange. The empire crumbled once its
mighty capital of Plutonia fell. Literally, into a giant whole, that's still
there today.

Nowadays, Brancalonia is officially a domain of the Empire of Altomagna whose
throne is somewhere in Central Occasia, but despite repeated attempts that
foreign empire has never managed to bring any sort of general order to the
place. The last one was a hundred years ago when the entire kingdom was given as
a bounty to mercenary general Menalda. Her reign was long, but she without
leaving a heir and her crown was lost.

The Bounty Kingdom remains, as it has for the last thousand years, as a
collection of squabbling city states and feudal domains who often conspire and
make war on each other. It's a paradise for mercenaries and swindlers, though it
is not a lawless frontier. It's also a magical land, where miracle-workers and
spellcasters of all types ply their trade, frightening monsters hide in out of
the way places, faeries influence mortal fates, and wooden puppets become
people.

The dominant religion here is the most Catholic fantasy Catholicism I've ever
seen. The Faith of the Calendar is technically monotheistic, but the part of if
that most people are familiar with are the many hundreds of Saints, at least one
for each day of the year, each with their own narrow purview, each once a mortal
that was canonized. There are four different people claiming to be its
Patriarch, two in Brancalonia and two others in different parts of the
world. Below the Patriarch you have the Ordinals and a bunch of lower priestly
rankings, but again the one most people are familiar with are the friars and
monks that can be seen wandering the countryside or studying in their
monasteries. You also have the older nature-based faiths of the pagans, which
still have a lot of adherents out in the sticks.

The book includes some details about the various regious of the peninsula, each
of which offers a slightly different variation of the genre conventions
described above. Some of them have more mystical weirdness going on, some are
centered on knightly contests, the canal-filled city of Vortiga is there if you
want urban intrigue, and so on. There's even an Italian Ravenloft here in the
form of Penumbria, a province surrounded by a deadly wall of mist that seems to
filter out the comedy to present a darker and more violent setting for Grand
Guignol-style horror.

## On Player Characters

As mentioned above, PCs in Brancalonia are known as Knaves. Character creation
and gameplay mostly uses the default D&D rules, but we have some important
modifications here to help maintain the intended atmosphere of "low-life
heroes in a (technically) low-magic world".

The most important one is that character advancement is limited to level 6. This
keeps PC powers within the 5e equivalent of "heroic tier". At level 6 a PC
becomes a "Knave Emmeritus" and abandons the standard leveling chart, though
they still might gain a few further power boosts from accumulating XP.

Resting also takes longer - 8 hours for short rests and a whole week for long
rests. This helps enforce a narrative structure for the game that alternates
missions for the Company with extended periods of downtime. They should be able
to complete a "typical" job without taking a long rest in the middle, though
some big ones might take longer.

Finally, characters start with 15 _silver_ pieces instead of gold to purchase
their starting equipment. This ties into the rules for _shoddy equipment_ we get
in the middle of the book: shoddy gear costs 10% of list price but has a chance
to fail in embarrassing ways when it's most needed. Weapons and armor can
spontaneously disassemble mid-fight, potions might make you ill, and
miscellaneous items (and even horses) will have their own foibles.

Now for the good stuff: we get a full spread of Brancalonia-specific ancestries
(still called "races" in this book) and subclasses, as well as several new
subsystems for activities that are expected to be common in Brancalonian games.

### Ancestries

Most characters in a Brancalonia game will belong to one of these. Players can
still opt to play as a more "standard" D&D ancestry like a dwarf or elf, but
those will also mean the character is a foreigner. May be they hail from Frange,
Great Brigantain, far-away Illusitania, or even more mysterious lands.

One of the jokes that doesn't land very well here is that every ancestry
described in the book has a section on "funny nicknames" (slurs) the others call
them. This is the kind of very 90s gaming humor that I'm not sure I'd use in my
games. Fortunately it's more or less contained to these paragraphs in the
ancestry descriptions and can easily be removed.

The setting is otherwise mostly free of fantasy racism. No matter their
ancestry, characters will usually belong to the dominant culture of their home
region, and there are also plenty of alternatives for non-dominant cultures that
are also multi-species.

We all know and love **humans**, of course. They get the usual flexible stat
bonuses and extra feat.

If you want to play someone like Fezzik, Obelix, or any character ever played by
Bud Spencer, you can be a **Morgant**. A human variant said to be distantly
descended from giants or ogres, they're stronger than baselines and enjoy many
of the benefits of being Large while still being technically Medium.

There's also the **Sylvans**, another human variant that's a little hairier than
usual and much more at home in the forests and wild regions of the
kingdoms. They're said to have lived here long before anyone else.

Finally there are the **Gifted**, people that have a touch of the arcane power
of Extravaganza in their veins, either inherited from their ancestors or from
direct exposure. They get a couple of innate spells and make excellent
sorcerers.

Then we have the two species that are completely non-human.

The **Marionettes** are child-sized artificial people made of _triflewood_
extracted from Extravaganza-rich trees. They behave much like Small living
constructs, with a couple of variants based on their intended purpose:
_Pinocchios_ are made for the stage and are more charismatic, while _Pupos_ are
more or less pint-sized Warforged.

And then you have the coolest tiefling-types in all of D&D. A standard D&D
tiefling is someone distantly descended from a fiend, who might have frequent
scenes where they lament their fate with phrases like "I didn't choose this." A
**Malebranche** is an actual devil who got tired of their infernal existence and
performed the Great Refusal, giving up most of their eternal memories and powers
in exchange for a mortal body and the ability to walk under the stars again. In
other words, they _walked backwards out of Hell while flipping off the Devil
with both hands_. A Malebranche looks at the body they designed themselves prior
to incarnating and proudly proclaims "I chose this!".

### Subclasses

Most characters in Brancalonia are expected to belong to one of these
subclasses, and might be referred to by their names even before the mechanical
benefits kick in depending on how they present themselves. They can of course
belong to other subclasses if they want to, but here is where converting from
standard D&D gets trickier, because of the level limit.

Barbarians can be _pagans_, representing one of the many communities of people
who lived here way before the Church and the Draconian Empire ever showed up,
and who still follow their old customs. They get a more controlled form of rage
that makes them difficult or impossible to pin down, depending on their level.

Bards can be _Harlequins_, who follow Commedia dell'Arte traditions instead of
the ones "standard" D&D bards are based on. The illustration here is notable for
showing Malebranche Roberto Benigni as the iconic Harlequin Bard. If you ever
wanted to know what a slapstick actually is, this is your subclass.

Clerics can be "_miraculists_", people whose faith lets them perform actual
miracles by invoking the names of the Saints. They have what it takes to be
canonized... after death, if the Church learns about them. You don't have to
also be an ordained priest to be a miraculist, mind you.

Druids can be _benandanti_, members of the mystical tradition that was
protecting people from monsters around these parts millennia before the Church
was a thing. They're particularly good at fighting undead.

Fighters can be _swordfighters_, proficient in Italian-style fencing.

Monks can be _friars_ from the Brawling Orders, who rely on brute strength
instead of acrobatics. If turning the other cheek doesn't work, turn your hand
on the other guy's cheek.

Paladins can be _knights-errant_, who either come from an impoverished noble
house or like to pretend they do. Their oaths demand that they travel to seek
adventure, audaciously defend people from injustice, punish the wrong-doings of
others, and make amends for their own. It's the Brancaleone oath.

Rangers can be _matadors_, specialized in capturing horrible monsters alive and
training them for sale to arenas an other shady customers.

Rogues can be _brigands_, and finally take on the role of those ubiquitous
bandits that jump out from roadside bushes every time an adventuring party tries
to travel somewhere.

Sorcerers can be "_supersticians_", who inherit Extravaganza-based powers and
use them to ward off bad luck.

Warlocks can be _jinxes_, who make pacts with the anthropomorphic
personification of misfortune and whose abilities can _cause_ bad luck.

Wizards can be _guiscards_, who come at this whole "arcane trickster" business
from the wizard side and are particularly proficient at atuning to magic items.

### Other Bric-a-Brac

We get a selection of backgrounds and feats here, which complement those from
the core books. A lot of them interact closely with the systems for Bounties we
discuss further down.

There are also feats! Each ancestry, even humans, gets a feat exclusive to
them. The others are mostly somewhat generic, though there are some hilarious
standouts. You could be an inheritor of the Ancient Culinary Art passed down
from your nona or be so good at fast-talking people you can verbally bamboozle
them mid-fight.

### Equipment

As mentioned above, the notion of shoddy equipment is very important here. All
the starting equipment of our PCs is likely to be shoddy. There's also
counterfeit equipment, which looks fine but is shoddy, though counterfeit
_papers_ work a bit differently and can actually be useful.

As for New Stuff, you could arm yourself with a martial mattock or shovel, or
avail yourself of the many tonics and concoctions that Brancalonians are
obsessed with (as seen in _Brancaleone's Army_ and _Asterix & Obelix_). I
particularly love that last part.

## D&D Stands for Dens & Downtime

As mentioned aboves, your PC party is a Band of Knaves that belongs to a
Company. That's a generic term that can refer to any of a large number of
organization types. The players decide its exact nature. The game assumes that
whatever that nature is, the Company can take on jobs from outside clients,
and has a NPC leader that's responsible for negotiating the fees and assigning
tasks to the Band.

The company also has a Den, a home base where the Kaves who are currently not
working a job can live and rest. Once they're done with a mission, they return
here and can invest their hard-earned loot on resting, partying, and upgrading
the Den. Those upgrades will in turn give them extra edges when they finally
take on the next Job.

Downtime (or "rollicking", since the book thinks "downtime" is too fancy) almost
always includes a long rest lasting a week, and the PCs can also choose to spend
extra weeks lying low to reduce heat from the authorities, or doing the thing
that's the entire reason why they chose this life in the first place: partying
hard! This is an awesome way to vaporize any money remaining from upgrades
between jobs, and leads to hilarious results randomly rolled from a specialized
table.

None of this will be big news for people used to Blades in the Dark or the like,
but it's nice seeing it in D&D.

### Bounty Management

All Knaves belong to that most honorable society of Bounty Brothers, meaning
they have a bounty on their heads and abide by certain traditions. Each starting
character rolls on a table of light to medium-severity crimes three times to see
what their initial rap sheet looks like. Each of these has an associated bounty
value, and the sum of those is the character's total bounty. It's almost
guaranteed that this will gradually increase over the course of the campaign.

A character's bounty acts as a kind of parallel XP track. The higher your bounty
level (there are five) the higher your reputation bonus. This applies to social
interactions where that reputation matters, and to those random revelry rolls,
improving your chances of getting a beneficial result.

The downside is that a higher bounty level also increases the chance of extra
bad things coming your way during a job, courtesy of the authorities, bounty
hunters, and assorted Bad People who decide to mess with you because of your
reputation. So if you don't think that rep bonus is worth the hassle, you can
try to get some of your crimes struck from your record, lowering your bounty and
leveling you down.

People who violate the code of the Brotherhood (basically by behaving like
murderhobos and/or trying to turn their fellows in for the bounty) are branded
Infamous, and vilified by all proper Bounty Brothers.

### Bar Fights and Other Diversions

Finally, we get new subsystems for stuff that is likely to happen pretty often
in a Brancalonian campaign: brawls and tavern games.

The Brawl rules are for those somewhat comedic fights that tend to break out in
taverns, where people shove and slap each other around and might end up battered
and bruised, but alive. It's kind of a parallel combat system, combined with
in-setting etiquette rules. Every PC gets some special Brawl-related ability in
addition to the usual D&D things, there's a list of Brawl moves, and a health
level system to determine how much punishment you can take before you're down
and out. NPC statistics for brawls are separate from standard monster stat
blocks.

The winners of a Brawl are allowed to claim a single trophy from the losing
side, usually a single coin or some other emotionally significant
memento. Brawling is brawling, combat is combat, and it's good manners to keep
them separate. Drawing a weapon or otherwise switching to lethal tactics during
a brawl turns it into a standard combat, with everyone starting out at full HP
and resources regardless of what went on before. It also ruins your
reputation. Doing this repeatedly is one of the sure-fire ways of becoming
Infamous.

We also have rules for a series of "Dive games", which as the name implies
usually happen on taverns. They include eating or drinking contests, card and
dice games, and that thing where four drunken idiots organize into
"knight/horse" pairs and joust with mops and brooms.

## Adventures

To finish things, we have a section containing a sampling of short adventures
for all levels of Knave. They're probably the best illustration of the intended
tone of the setting, as only one or two of the seven presented here include
unavoidable standard D&D combats. Two others include the possibility as a kind
of "soft" fail state. It gets the job done, but it's a worse solution than any
other plan the PCs might come up with.

The adventures are much less structured than what you'd find in an official
Wizards of the Coast module, but that's okay. The GM is given enough information
to run each one, with plot overviews, personality descriptions for important
NPCs, and plenty of freedom to decide how to solve each cental problem in most
cases. They range from a kid-friendly trek up a fey mountain to bring a fairy
back to a corrupt merchant (who's in for a nasty surprise even if the PCs
succeed), to a trip to Hell in order to interrogate a dead witch about which of
her three children was sired by the local baron and is therefore his heir.

## Final Impressions

As you might have already guessed, I love this book. Despite its flaws, and
despite using a rules system that's far from my favorite, it gives me a setting
that practically sets my imagination on fire, and that often manages to pull
genuine laughter out of me.

If I ever run a game to a group again, it's going to be in this setting. And if
I ever use something like the D&D rules to do it, it's going to be the variant
from the Dragon Heresy Introductory set, which covers a similar level range and
would therefore mix pretty well.
