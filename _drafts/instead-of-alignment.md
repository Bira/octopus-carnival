One of the things I like about ICON, Massif Press' unreleased fantasy RPG, is
the way in which it classifies the enemies PCs might face in combat. Instead of
reaching for D&D style alignments and other reductionist classifications, it
divides potential opponents into two broader categories: **monsters** and
**kin**. I'm inclined to use this for my own games, with a few
adjustments. Here's how I'd do it.

## Kin

Kin are people!

- They always have complex reasons to fight.
- They can always be bargained with.
- They won't kill you if they don't have to.
- They'll run or surrender to avoid dying.

This means it's always possible to avoid a fight with kin by giving them what
they want, though doing so might not always be _desirable_. Cliche bandits want
to take your money. Those castle guards that caught you sneaking into the
treasure (or the prince's) chambers want to throw you in a cell.

You'll probably end up fighting them anyway because you don't want to let them
do those things, but you won't have to kill them all to win. Maybe you won't
even have to kill any of them! And if _they_ win, odds are they won't kill you
either.

You might also be able to talk them out of fighting through various means. Bribe
your way out of the castle, scare the bandits off, befriend the mushroom people
living in that dungeon so you can buy their special liquor in bulk instead of
robbing their homes...

Every one of the classic D&D "humanoids" falls into the "kin"
category. Non-humanoid creatures that are free-willed and can communicate are
also kin. That includes intelligent dragons, sphinxes, flumphs, Deep Ones, and
other such beings.

## Monsters

Monsters are the complete opposite of kin.

- They have very simple reasons to fight (hunger, territory, programming...).

- They cannot be bargained with.

- They attack on sight and fight to the death.

Monsters lack a sense of self-preservation, which contributes to their "fight to
the death" behavior. A "monster" that does show such sense is probably not a
monster at all but a Critter (see below). The only way to avoid a fight with a
monster is by running away or avoiding it in some fashion - you can't talk them
out of violence.

For the most part, monsters are _made_, not born. There might be magic involved,
or just "mundane" social issues caused by the cruelty of kin. Example monsters
are bound guardian spirits or constructs, nonsapient undead, incarnate
nightmares arising from collective trauma, a Critter driven to desperation by
destruction of its habitat, and other greeblies.

Monsters usually aren't sapient, but they might be. That bound guardian ghost
could be quite friendly as long as you don't cross the threshold to its
protected area - then it's compelled to kill you.

Perhaps the only truly "natural" monsters are slimes and oozes, who are driven
by instinct to dissolve any organic matter they can sense and are too stupid to
retreat.

## Critters

This third category doesn't appear on ICON. Instead, it's something I'm going to
introduce to cover that nebulous middle ground between monsters and kin that
always gets brought up in online discussions when I gush about this system. This
is the category for your big predators and other dangerous animals.

Like monsters, critters have very simple motivations. They want to eat when
they're hungry, sleep when they're tired, keep themselves and their territory
safe, and take care of their young. The priority order for these might vary with
the specific critter's instincts.

Like kin, however, they don't really want to kill you, even if the critter in
question is a predatory animal. It turns out that humanoids aren't typically
part of any realistic predator's menu, particularly if those humanoids are a
party of well-armed adventurers. A critter will fight you if it feels you're
threatening its life and it has no way to escape, and it will run away as soon
as it can. Otherwise it's content to keep its distance from you.

While critters are typically nonsapient, you can still "negotiate" with them
using animal handling skills to befriend them or at least convince them you're
too scary to fight.
