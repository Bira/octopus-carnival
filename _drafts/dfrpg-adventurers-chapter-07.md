We already went through the basics in chapter 1, character creation in chapters
2 and 3, and a whirlwind tour of the advantages, disadvantages and skills in
chapters 4, 5, and 6. Now it's time for the chapter covering equipment and
treasure.

Being a GURPS variant that can change whatever they want, DFRPG didn't _have_ to
go hard on the detail for encumbrance and gear. Yet they chose to do so anyway,
because according to our introduction in this chapter resource management is a
traditional part of the genre that many find fun. What corners will you cut in
consumables like arrows, potions and food in order to get that nifty weapon or
armor? What will you choose to leave behind to drag that sack of treasure back
to town? How smug will you feel when you realize you packed everything you
needed and nothing you didn't to that expedition?

If that sounds fun to you, you're in the right game! If it didn't, well, at
least you've been warned. Our first section is about buying and selling gear, so
we're going in order of importance here.

## Money Money Money!

The symbol used for money here is the "$". In standard GURPS they call this the
"GURPS dollar", and explain it means different things in each setting. In the
DFRPG, it means "one copper coin".

Dungeon Fantasy coins are, not coincidentally, identical to the ones used by D&D
and Pathfinder. There are 50 of these chonky disks to the pound no matter what
they're made of. Unlike D&D of PF coins however, their values here increase by a
factor of 20. So if a copper coin is $1, a silver coin is $20, and a gold coin
is $400. Gold coins are also valuable and soft enough that they're commonly cut
into halves, quarters or eights. Gold packs a lot more value into the same
weight, which is why delvers love it so much.

There are coins made of weirder metals with in-between values, but those are not
in this book. You might find them as treasure, though.

Every PC starts with $1000 to buy their starting gear. This is modified by the
PC's Wealth. PCs who have that as an advantage get more, those who have it as a
disadvantage get less. They can also exchange character points for extra money
at $500 per point - a common use for those 5 points obtained from quirks.

### Buying

Item prices assume the most typical conditions possible - a typical sale by an
ordinary merchant at a time and place where there's neither a surplus not a
shortage. Everything costs list price at character creation, and campaign events
might change that. The Exploits book will describe some tricks you can try to
get stuff cheaper.

If you really like an item you can spend character points on the Signature Gear
advantage to give it some narrative protection. That advantage costs 1 point per
$500 of the item's value.

Delvers usually live out of inns and such, so it costs $150 a week to live in
town. Again there are tricks to reduce this in Exploits.

### Selling

You don't need to sell coins, of course - they're already money. Everything else
will sell in town for a percentage of its "true" value that's proportional to
the Wealth of the character doing the selling. That trait also representes the
quality of the PC's abstract business contacts, you see. Average-wealth PCs, the
default, sell loot at 40% of its value.

## Weapons

Every single PC is going to need at least one weapon. Which one depends on their
combat skills, and what they can both carry, wield, and afford. This section
goes on to explain weapon stats, and then gives us the big weapon tables.

The Melee Weapons Table gives us everything you'd expect out of a dungeon
fantasy game.

- Axes and Maces are cheap and have decent damage but tend to be unbalanced -
  you can attack _or_ parry with them, but not both. The two-handed varieties
  have high damage but are more expensive, heavier, and so unbalanced you might
  need to spend a whole turn readying the weapon after attacking with it.

- Swords of all kinds are balanced and versatile, with both swinging and
  thrusting attacks. They are however, very expensive. You can use the same
  skills to wield balanced clubs and staffs if you don't have the cash for a
  real blade, however.

- Knives are cheap, but their short blades give them relatively poor damage and
  a defense penalty. When wielded with fencing skills, their damage remains poor
  but their defensive ability increases by a lot.

- Fencing skills can also be used with several types of specialized sword like
  rapiers or smallswords. These tend to have slightly worse damage than other
  swords and be more expensive, but give fantastic defensive bonuses when used
  with the right skills.

- Spears and polearms have excellent damage-to-price ratios but are often
  unbalanced and heavy, requiring two hands to use.

- Staffs can be wielded like swords or spears, and also with their own skill
  that gives a better defensive bonus.

- Flails and nunchakus are hard to use and unbalanced, but they're also hard to
  defend against.

The Ranged Weapon Table has everything you expect and some things you don't.

- Bows of all sizes have excellent range, with damage ranging from middling to
  great as their size increases. Price also increases accordingly.

- Crossbows are heavier and take longer to reload, but do more damage.

- Slings are hard to use but do damage based on the character's swing damage,
  which is higher.

- We have many types of thrown weapon: spears, axes, knives, shuriken, darts,
  even boomerangs. Some of these can also be used in melee and appear on those
  tables as well.

- Spear-throwers are devices that extend the range of thrown spears and let
  their damage use the PC's swing value instead of their thrust.

- And you also have some oddballs: lassos, nets, and blowpipes.
