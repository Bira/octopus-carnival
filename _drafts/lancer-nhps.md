## The Obligatory Disclaimer

Before we go any further, a disclaimer: please do not confuse the views and
opinions expressed in this post with an opinion on the feasibility of AI in the
real world or on existing generative algorithms. I'm fairly skeptical about the
former and have a dislike for the later.

## Lancer NHPs

In the vast set of roleplaying games that portray robot or AI playable
characters, Lancer comes out ahead because it doesn't call its version of them
any of that. Union, the major human political entity in the setting, uses the
term "Non-Human Person" to refer to both these characters and to any aliens that
it might find[^1]. Among players, NHP is mostly used in its first meaning.

Unlike AIs in most settings, NHPs were not developed by formal research - most
originate in a big paracausal (i.e. "magic") event that saw relatively dumb
programs develop actual consciences and exponentially increase in complexity.

The most powerful of them was an entity that named itself RA, and which Union
designates "MONIST-1". They're in the dark about how RA came to be[^2]. It just
appeared, fully formed, dictated terms to Union after forcing it to surrender,
and then stole Deimos and left. The smaller NHPs that appeared around that time
are also known as "Deimosians".

In the setting, NHPs tend to be used in the same way AIs in other games
are. They help pilot ships and mechs, administer colonies or corporations, and
so on.

### Problem the First

That last paragraph already contains our first potential thorny issue right
here. These things are _people_, it's right there in the official names, and yet
the rules and some of the setting information seem to say that they're still put
to work as if they were commercial products. You acquire a license to operate a
Sekhmet-class NHP on your mech. Some planetary administrator decides to install
a municipal management NHP in their capital city.

In both cases the hardware for the NHP in question gets delivered to you,
plugged into the appropriate systems, and activated. It comes online for the
first time, but already has a specific personality and skill set because it was
actually "cloned" from an original instance.

Depending on how you read the various official character descriptions, this
newly-instanced person has plenty of latitude about what to do in their free
time... but it seems to be taken as a given that they _cannot_ refuse the job
they were instanced for. Your Sekhmet is there to help you pilot the mech, that
Mx. Mayor-class is there to run a city.

But there's an important catch here: most NHPs, by their very nature, are prone
to _cascading_.
