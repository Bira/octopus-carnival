Brancalonia is a setting published in 2021 for Dungeons & Dragons 5th Edition by
Italian publisher Acheron. It was the product of a successful Kickstarter
campaign, and exists in both the original Italian and an English translation,
which is the version we'll be looking at here.

I'm from Brazil and I don't speak Italian, but I happen to live in a region of
Brazil whose culture and idioms were strongly shaped by waves of Italian
immigration in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. The bits of Italian I do
understand translate more directly to Portuguese than English does, and a lot of
the idioms and jokes used there have direct equivalents here. So a lot of the
humor in this setting lands for me in ways that don't really come through in
English. I have the Italian quick-start, and I'll be referencing it and trying
to explain the jokes when appropriate.


## What Is the Book About?

Brancalonia is a setting based on Italian folklore and on a particular kind of
Italian fantasy fiction. The English translation calls it "Spaghetti Fantasy" in
a clear reference to Spagetthi Western movies, but from what I understand it
that's not really the right vibe at all.

As the book quickly tells us in its introduction, the bedrock on which its build
is the Italian movie _L'Armata Brancaleone_, known as _The Incredible Army of
Brancaleone_ or _For Love and Gold_ in English. Even the name of the setting
hints at this. It's about adventures in a medieval setting, but instead of
starring noble knights and mighty wizards, it centers on a bunch of peniless
scallywags trying to stay alive, have some fun, and maybe strike it rich.

In other words, you don't play the famous heroes, you play the people they call
to actually solve problems when the heroes are too busy grandstanding.

Youtube provides us with [a clip][1] of such a party getting ready to start
their next scheme/adventure. Even if you, like me, can't understand Italian,
you'll get a pretty good idea of the look and feel that the book builds on. The
guy on the horse is Brancaleone himself, and the guy on Brancalonia's cover is
clearly based on him.

## Cover and Introduction

The first thing I noticed right on the cover is that the art in this book is
_fantastic_. The style is different from what I've come to expect from D&D
books, and it works very well for this one. The page backgrounds and main text
font are pretty standard for 5e books, but they're interspersed with gorgeous
pencil illustrations and the occasional full color piece.

The introduction covers the same things I talked about in the previous section,
but in greater detail, and with a much greater inspirational media
selection. Brancaleone is there, of course, but so are Corto Maltese, Pinocchio,
lots of books by Italo Calvino, and _Baudolino_ by Umberto Eco.

## Chapter 1: A World of Knaves

This short chapter explains who the PCs are and how the overall tone of the
setting is reflected in the rules.

Instead of "adventurers", PCs are _Knaves_, members of a company of
"mercenaries, rogues, and similar rascals". Companies are composed of _bands_,
which are groups of Knaves who have proved to be compatible enough that they
won't try to murder each other a few steps down the road when sent on a
job. Small companies have a handful of bands, while larger ones might have a few
hundred and can be quite influential.

Bands have a lot of freedom to roam the land, and are often hired by villagers
merchants, nobles, and warlords to do the sort of job no one else wants to
do. On a good day, those are merely illicit or dangerous. On a bad one they
might involve demons, curses, and all sorts of monsters. A lot of specific Band
examples appear here, ranging from a group of friars chasing a holy relic to the
crew of a pirate ship.

There's a sidebar on alignment here that really encourages players to ensure
their Knaves have hearts of gold, with good or neutral alignments if those are
used in the campaign. This tends to match the tone of the source material. Evil
Knaves obviously exist, but playing them is discouraged.

The Italian term for PCs is _Canaglie_, which translates to "canalhas" in
Portuguese and feels a lot heavier than all of the other English terms here, at
least to me. However even this is still "lighter" than being a murderhobo, which
the rules later on try to discourage.

In Brancalonia, the GM is the _Condottiero_. This is also how the historical
captains of Italian mercenary companies were called, which is a nice touch. The
leader of the PC's company is kind of the GM's own PC, interacting with them
directly when it's time to assign jobs and the like.

### Low-Life Heroes

In order to keep to the scruffy low-magic tone of the source material,
Brancalonia games only go up to level 6. There are ways to keep advancing after
that, but they're slower and less powerful than traditional level-ups. As a nice
touch, there's an old-fashioned table here associating each level with a named
rank, topping out at "Knave Emeritus" at Level 6.

### Importing Knaves

Again to keep with the spirit of the source material, the book encourages
players to make their PCs using only the species, subclasses, and backgrounds
found inside, with the classes from the PHB. Still, players who really want to
play a dwarf, elf, or even a foreigner[^1] can do so.

Subclasses from other sources require some examination before they can be
allowed, however. Overly "heroic" or "grimdark" ones are outside of the intended
tone, and they might also not be a good mechanical fit with the rules specific
to Brancalonia like the limited advancement and others we'll see soon.
