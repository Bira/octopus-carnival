In this post I'm going to tackle a question that I think has been kind of
looming in the background of Star Wars stories: "Are droids people?"

The last time I saw this discussion online, the participants went straight to a
philosophical argument about whether real-world computer programs could achieve
sentience, and what sentience even is, and so on. You know the one. This was
before the AI craze of the past few years, but it's the same kind of talk that
gets deployed to argue whether generative algorithms are "true AI" or not.

I'm not here to talk about that. First, because I don't think large language
models are or can ever be people. And second because Star Wars is not only a
fictional setting, it's one where spaceships go vroom-vroom and pew-pew in the
vacuum of space, while being piloted by space wizards in flowing robes with
magic swords strapped to their belts. Citing real-world science when talking
about it means you've already started out wrong.

The answer here is all about their _narrative role_. And since a lot of Star
Wars droids are actual characters with names, personalities and individual
voices [^1], it makes no sense to answer the question with anything other than
"yes" for them.

You also have droids in incidental roles. These don't get names and might not
get lines. You see them either walking around or performing some sort of
specialized job. And finally you have the battle droids from the prequel movies,
who appear in large numbers to fight the good guys and commit war atrocities
while spitting out goofy dialogue.

Even _those_ droids are people,


I'm looking at droids here as fictional characters in a fantastic narrative, not
as any kind of analogy to real-life robots and the sort of thing people are
calling AI today. That's my attitude to all fictional robots, but it's
particularly relevant in a setting like Star Wars that has never even paid lip
service to real science.

So, _are_ droids people? A lot of the franchise's more beloved characters are
droids. They have names, they have personalities, they have voices[^1], they
have distinct roles in the narrative. All of this combines make my answer to the
question a big "yes".

Therefore I was a bit shocked when I saw an online discussion about Star Wars
where someone _didn't_ think droids were people. To this person, they were just
funny appliances, and it was right and proper that they were treated as
such. And I guess they arrived at that conclusion because a lot of the time
that's how the other characters in the story treat droids. And not just villains
either, but protagonists as well.

That starts happening right from A New Hope, where our buddies Artoo and
Threepio are captured by Jawas and bought by Luke and his uncle, who needed a
couple of droids for their moisture farm. This is problematic because the
narrative role the droid duo has is one that's filled by peasants and commoners
in The Hidden Fortress, the story that movie is based on. And of course, robots
in fiction are almost always a stand-in for human workers. "Luke buys two
peasants to work on his farm" doesn't sound like a very heroic thing, now does
it?



[^1]: Even when you can't understand what they're saying.
