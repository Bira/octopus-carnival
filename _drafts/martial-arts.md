There's a very long thread in the RPG.net forum where some users ask questions
about martial arts, and other users answer them. Some of the question-askers in
that thread have a view I found a bit peculiar: they seem to view martial arts
styles as being kind of like character classes. This became evident in the
nature of the questions, which were like "which style is better at doing this
specific thing?", and at some statements like "kung-fu was proved to be
useless".

I guess this styles-as-classes views could be a consequence of most of one's
prior knowledge coming from RPGs, because a lot of them hold that very same
view. But it's not restricted to tabletop players, I think. I remember seeing
videos from actual martial arts practitioners (who are certainly a lot better at
it than me) that seemed to kinda hold similar views.

The most recent example I saw was by this guy named Rokas, who practiced Aikido
for 14 years and was dismayed at the results when he tried sparring with MMA
practitioners. He completely abandoned aikido and switched to competitive MMA
training. However, after some years of this, he found out some movements from
aikido actually worked really well once he applied them with the additional
experience that came from a few years of sparring and actual competitive fights
against resisting opponents. And he wondered if this was why the founder of
Aikido required his students to already know how to fight before he began
teaching them.

The "kung fu is useless" thing probably comes from this series of videos by a
Chinese guy who practices MMA and/or Sanda and has a habit of challenging
masters of traditional kung fu styles to public matches. He pounds them into
dust, of course, but I wouldn't say that the reason this happens is that "kung
fu is useless" and you're wasting your time on a "bad build" by learning
it. There's more nuance there.

The first bit of nuance comes from the already traditional answer that people
experienced with martial arts give to those who ask "what style is best?". It's
another question: "best for what?". Are you only intending to keep fit and maybe
improve your balance and coordination? Do you want to take part in sports
competitions? If so, what type of competition? Do you want to start fights on
the street (please don't!). Do you want to defend yourself from the people who
answered "yes" to the previous question?

The second bit comes from the realization that martial arts themselves are
mutable. There isn't a Great Supplement in the Sky that encodes the eternal and
unchanging form of each style. Even a traditional style, where you can trace the
student-teacher lineage back through the centuries, has been going through
incremental changes all the while. This happens because each teacher has a
different take on it, but also because goals change over time.

In other words, martial arts styles aren't character classes. They're a very
long game of telephone.


## Style-as-class in Games

The biggest of them all is of course D&D, which literally assigns an
entirely separate class to each different fighting style starting from about 3.x
onwards.

Other popular game systems also have martial arts styles as isolated rule
packages with all the hallmarks of a class even if the system is otherwise
point-based. This means that each one requires a high point investment, has its
own set of abilities you learn as you get better, and learning an additional
style means you have to start from scratch. The games that come to mind here are
the classic World of Darkness with its Combat supplement, Chronicles of
Darkness, Shadowrun 4th Edition with its Arsenal supplement, Cyberpunk 2020,
and Exalted. I'm sure there are others.

The degree to which different styles can be mixed varies between
games. Generally speaking, the more recent the game, the easier it is. Cyberpunk
2020 has each style be an entirely separate combat skill with a different point
cost and different bonuses. Shadowrun and Chronicles of Darkness make styles
into separate leveled advantages that give bonuses to a certain combat skill and
let you mix those bonuses freely. Exalted sits between those for its first two
editions, and does a hard backwards turn in 3rd where it makes each one a
separate skill with powers that are utterly incompatible with both other martial
arts and with the "normal" powers PCs in that system get.

It comes as no surprise to anyone who reads this blog that my preferred approach
is that of GURPS 4th Edition, by way of its GURPS Martial Arts supplement. GURPS
is a fairly old and traditional system, with even 4th Ed being 20 years old now,
but it doesn't do style-as-class at all. Its martial arts styles are a very
"light" investment - each has required skills which a combat focused-character
would already have as a matter of course, and then a 1-point familiarity
advantage that mitigates a few penalties from someone using the same style on
you. "Getting better" at a style mostly means increasing your combat skills,
which are common to all of them. There are some associated perks and techniques,
but they're all optional, and far from exclusive.

This means learning more styles becomes easier as you become a better fighter,
mixing and matching is pretty much trivial, and a fighter's individual skill
matters a heck of a lot more than what styles they know. I love that.


## Style-as-class in Real Life

The real life mirror of the style-as-class attitude is of course the ages-old
arguments about what style is "best". People have been having those for
thousands of years, and a lot of the myths associated with particular martial
arts come from their ancient practitioners bragging about what they could do.

Nowadays, someone that has real-life martial arts experience and isn't trying to
sell you something will usually anwer the "what style is best" question with
another one: "best at what?" The answer will change depending on what the asker
wants to do. Is it just for fitness purposes? Is it for sports matches? Is it
for self-defense?

This line of reasoning is a very good starting point, but there's some more
nuance in there that might take longer to grasp, I think.
