Way back when I was a kid, I remember reading a version of Don Quixote "for
kids", where the language of the story was vastly simplified and the events were
kinda summarized[^1]. This year, I got the full version as a birthday gift from
my family.

I went through its introductions and the first couple of chapters and arrived at
the conclusion that while the book is an enjoyable read, it's also the kind of
book that is better enjoyed when you made notes as you read along. So here we
are.

## What is this?

The smallest summary I can give you is that **Don Quixote** is a story about a
guy who read way so much isekai he became The Hero in his own mind.

The slightly longer version is that **Don Quixote** is a novel published in two
parts by 17th-century Spanish author Miguel de Cervantes. Part 1 was published
in 1605. Part 2 was written later and published in 1615. The edition I got here
is a _hefty_ single volume, translated to Brazilian Portuguese by two people
with actual noble titles (the Count of Azevedo and the Viscount of Castilho). So
I guess I can expect the footnotes and explanations to be, er, a little less
critical of the idea of nobility than I am.

This is one of the most famous novels in the world. Cervantes is arguably a
bigger deal than Shakespeare.


## Foreword

The edition I have has a foreword by Célia Navarro Flores, who has a doctorate
in literature from the University of São Paulo and teaches the subject at the
University of Sergipe. She likes to joke that you can divide Western literature
in two periods: before Cervantes and after Cervantes. But why is this book such
a big deal?

In it Cervantes introduces a bunch of never-before-seen techniques: talking
about literature in a work of literature, talking directly to the reader,
multiple and sometimes contradictory viewpoints, a story that is in a
conversation with other stories and so on.

The "talking about literature" bit comes right at the prologue, where Cervantes
says he wrote this book to combat the "pernicious influence" of chivalric
romances, which were all the rage back then. These were mostly fantastical
stories about valorous knights that went around imaginary lands interacting with
kings and queens, and fighting foes like giants, monsters and wizards. Cervantes
talks shit about these books at several points, especially in the scene where
Quixote's friends stage an intervention by burning the books they judge made him
mad[^2].

The multiple viewpoints are of course related to how Don Quixote sees a
four-armed giant when his squire Sancho only sees a windmill, or how you can
never really know who Dulcinea really is because everyone has a different
opinion about her character.

And the bit about being in "conversation with other stories" will sound
immensely familiar because it happens all the time today. You see, Part 1 was
published in 1605. In 1614 some guy writing under a pseudonym published a
continuation of that book, with a preface that attacked Cervantes directly. Yes,
he wrote fanfic to "fix" the original author's story.

Cervantes got Big Mad about it, so he wrote and published part 2 in 1615, with a
preface that insulted the other guy back, a chapter where Quixote made the
fanfic's protagonist sign a document stating there was only one Don Quixote and
one Sancho, and a closing statement saying Don Quixote will die so that no one
else can write continuation fanfic about him.

Flores talks about how the books were considered funny comedy yarns when they
were published and for a few centuries afterwards, and how in the 19th century
people began analyzing them more deeply and find a bunch of interesting
allusions to the human condition, with Don Quixote representing the more
idealistic dreamer half of the human soul and Sancho its more down-to-earth,
practical side. She finishes with a big list of works in Brazilian and
international literature that were influenced by this book and the themes
contained within it.

## Prologue

Here, Cervantes explains that he isn't the "father" of this novel, but its
"stepfather", since as the footnotes explain us the novel has a framing
device. He's supposedly relating to us a real story found in historical records
of the Spanish region of Mancha.

Because of this, he doesn't have the rose-tinted glasses with which a lot of
authors view their work, so he's very worried that it won't be
well-received. It's just an accounting of facts. It doesn't have all the trendy
flourishes like quotations from great classical philosophers or the Bible,
erudite marginalia, or an initial section full of sonnets penned by nobles.

He hesitated the longest time before publishing the book, but then a Good
Friend[^3] came into the room as he was agonizing over the prologue. Cervantes
explained the situation and Friend said he shouldn't sweat it. Just compose
those poems himself and attribute them to someone important who can't be
interviewed about them. And here's a bunch of one-size-fits-all quotes you can
add to your margins at appropriate points. One for lovey-dovey scenes, another
one when things get sad, and so on. Don't worry about getting their authors
wrong, no one's gonna check (and indeed, the footnotes say that like half the
quotes Friend mentions are already misattributed).

## Preliminary verses

And here are those poems Friend talked about. We get the original verses in
old-ish Spanish, and then a Portuguese translation. I won't bother reproducing
them here, but it's a funny collection because they're all from characters of
other chivalric romances, addressed to the characters in this book.

- One from Urganda the Unknown, a witch from the Amadis de Gaula stories,
  to the book itself. "Don't throw stones at your neighbors when your roof is
  made of glass."

- A bunch of sonnets and other short poems supposedly written by the heroic
  protagonists, devoted squires, and beautiful damsels of other chivalric
  romances to their counterparts in this one, essentially saying things like
  "you're just like me fr fr" and "you're so awesome I'm jealous".

- A dialogue between Rocinante and another knight's stalwart stallion. "Why are
  you so scruffy? / Because I barely eat. / Ask your masters for more food. /
  It's no use, they're scruffier than me."

That last one is one of the instances where the actual nobles translating this
made Rocinante's insults before the quoted exchange a little lighter in tone
than they seemed to be in the original.

## Chapter 1

We start with the description about a certain hidalgo from La Mancha. "Hidalgo"
can be unpacked and translated to mean "son of something", and it refers to
someone who belongs to a hereditary noble lineage.

Wikipedia tells me that nobility in Spain at this time was a little more
complicated than what appears in your typical D&D setting. Being a hidalgo was
kinda orthogonal to having a title. Someone could have a non-hereditary title,
and someone could be a hidalgo without a title to go with it. They could own
land, bear arms, have heraldry, be judged by special courts, and so on. You can
consider a hidalgo without a title to be the lowest noble rank, in effect. Our
guy was one of these.

His name was probably something like Quijana. A tall and thin fellow, he owned a
small farm and lived with a governess, his niece, and an employee who took care
of all the farm work. He's well off, being a landowner and all, but he doesn't
have a lot of liquidity and so he only barely manages to keep the appearances
required by his status. He dressed fastidiously in slightly antiquated outfits,
had some very old arms and armor hanging from a stand in a corner of the house,
a very thin and sick horse, and his meals are relatively unadorned. When our
story starts he's fifty years old, which is unremarkable today but in the 1600s
meant he was kinda ancient.

Our pal used to go on hunts as a hobby, but in recent years he got into reading
chivalric romances. Like _really_ into them.

Quijana's favorite novels were pretty much the trashiest ones. The purpler the
prose, the more he liked them. And I muse stress he got _really_ into
them. Wrote fanfic. Had extensive arguments with the local priest and
barber-surgeon from the nearby village over which knight was the best. Gave up
hunting and started slacking on his administrative work on the farm. Sold off
bits of fertile land to buy more books.

Quijana likes heroic knight-errants so much that one day he decides he's going
to be one. He grabs those old and rusty weapons, which actually belonged to his
great-grandfather, and cleans them up as best he can. There's a bit here where
he gets upset that his helmet is only a simple skullcap, so he takes some
cardboard and turns it into a greathelm with a visor and everything. When that
falls apart on the first stress test, he adds some metal bars to the cardboard
and calls it good enough.

He takes a look at his horse, as mentioned before is far from the strongest or
healthiest beast around. To Quijana, though, he looks like the most awesome
horse in all of human history, so he renames it Rocinante, which to him sounds
glorious but is more or less the equivalent of naming your scruffy horse
"Scrufflord".

And since his horse has such an awesome name now, he spends another week coming
up with a name for himself, Don Quixote. "Don" is a honorific like "sir" for
knights, and "quixote" is the piece of armor that goes over your thighs. And
since he's from Mancha and heroic knights like to tack on the name of their
homeland to their own, he becomes Don Quixote de La Mancha.

With his cosplay game on point and the awesome names all decided, Don Quixote
needs only one other thing to complete his regalia: a lady to pine for. Someone
to whom he can send trophies of his victories and whose beauty he can loudly
proclaim to the four winds. And it just so happens that there's this woman he
saw once in the nearby village of Toboso and thought was cute. Her name is
Aldonza Lorenzo, which is way too common, so he mentally renames her to the much
more awesome Dulcinea del Toboso. Obviously, he doesn't inform her or anyone
else of this.

## Chapter 2

And so it was that Don Quixote considered himself ready to sally forth for the
first time, in search of adventure. He decides to do it very quietly before the
crack of dawn so his niece and governess can't stop him. He puts on his suit of
plate with the cosplay helmet, arms himself with lance, sword and shield, and
rides Rocinante out of the farm's back gate.

He almost gives up when he remembers he doesn't actually have the title of
knight. You can't just decide you're one, a full-fledged knight has to give you
the title in a special ceremony. Well, finding someone who can knight him will
be his first quest! He'll also find some time to wipe the old markings from his
shield so he can paint it with his own personalized crest when he earns one
through his deeds.

As he rides on, Don Quixote begins to imagine all the awesome adventures he will
have. At some point he's bound to find a sage who will decide to follow him and
chronicle his deeds, which is the framing device all those novels use, and so he
more or less begins composing his own novel right there, imagining what he'd
dictate to this sage. The prose he comes up with is purpler than the sky at
dawn.

What actually happens is that he rides the whole day without meeting anyone else
on the road. By this time both him and Rocinante are getting pretty hungry,
thirsty and tired, since they did nothing but travel all day. And of course he
doesn't have any food or water on him, the books never said knights needed any
of these things. Whenever they need rest, there's always a handy castle around
whose inhabitants are more than happy to provide room and board for the hero.

And it just so happens he sees a shabby inn in the distance, so he immediately
imagines it's a fabulous castle and approaches it. The inn's usual clientele are
donkey caravans transporting goods between towns - the animals go into the
sizable stable, the workers and whoever is traveling with them into the
rooms. The latter include two young sex workers who are now hanging out near the
entrance to take in the night air. Don Quixote approaches them and addresses
them like fair noble maidens with the full force of his poetical abilities,
which means they can't understand a word he's saying and can't help laughing at
him.

He begins to get a little angry, but then the innkeeper steps in. This guy is a
rogue, but a clever one. He immediately clocks Quixote as delusional, but in an
_entertaining_ fashion. So when Don Quixote addresses him as if he was the
senior knight who owned this fantastical castle, the innkeeper plays along and
brings him in, signaling the ladies to play along as well.

They strip him of his armor but can't take his helmet off. The cloth ribbons
that tie the (cosplay) visor to the rest of the helmet are tied with the most
annoyingly tight sort of blind knot. They'd have to be cut, and he forbids
that. So we're treated to the image of an unarmored Don Quixote with a full helm
still on, having to be fed stew and wine through straws by the two
maidens. This is exactly the kind of show the innkeeper hoped for.

Don Quixote later talks to the "senior knight" and asks to be knighted. The
innkeeper says of course, and tells a few made up tall tales of his own
"knightly erring" days. Cool, let's do the full ceremony, where is the chapel so
Don Quixote can spend the knight standing vigil over his arms? Oh, we had to
demolish it for renovations, but you can stand in the courtyard, it's not a
problem. So off he goes, placing his armor and weapons around the water pump as
if it was an altar, and marching around them in vigil.

The thing about a courtyard water pump is that those caravan workers need to use
it to water their animals, which is something that needs to happen right after a
caravan arrives, and can happen even in the middle of the night. No less than
two guys come in during the vigil, see a bunch of junk around the pump, and
move it aside. Don Quixote gets big mad at these ruffians profaning his weapons,
so he picks up his lance and whacks them over the head with it.

By the time the innkeeper arrives there's a whole caravan throwing stones at Don
Quixote, who keeps himself covered with his shield while challenging the
villains to fight him in melee. He manages to calm down the situation, cut the
vigil short, and do a "field knighting" right then and there so Don Quixote can
go away.

## Chapter 3

Don Quixote is once again erring around the countryside, but this time it
doesn't take long until he runs into more Adventure. On a forest clearing just
to the side of the road, he finds a torture scene. One guy is tied to a tree
while another one applies a lash to his back.

He sees a cruel knight torturing his squire and goes to challenge him and order
him to release the victim. The reality is pretty close to this. The victim is a
farmhand, and the torturer his boss. They're Don Quixote's neighbors,
actually.

The farmer tries to defend himself by saying the farmhand is a "no good
lazybones" who's bad at his job and had the gall to ask for a raise. The
farmhand cries for help and says that the only thing that's true about that is
the last bit, which was reason enough for this shitty penny-pincher of a boss to
resort to the lash.

Don Quixote, to his credit, immediately sides with the farmhand and demands that
the boss release him if he doesn't want a fight. Then Don Quixote has him swear
"on his knightly honor" to give his employee the pay raise he asked for, and to
stop treating him like a beast. The farmhand gets pretty happy at this!

Unfortunately that knightly honor exists only in Don Quixote's head, and as soon
as the old knight is out of sight, the boss re-ties the victim and resumes his
lashing. It will be some time before Don Quixote meets these two again.

For now, he's traveling once more, happy that he saw justice served. Then he
reaches a crossroads and forgets all about the previous situation. Quixote
remembers that in his novels you could often find knights planted at crossroads
who'd stop travelers and demand they praised his lady, challenging all who
refused to a honor duel. He thinks that's wicked cool, so he decides to spend
some time living the dream and being that knight.

Soon after starts his vigil, a merchant caravan approaches. It's a fairly large
one, with a few well-to-do merchants riding along with a large staff of servants
and teamsters. Don Quixote bars their way and loudly demands that they swear on
their honor that Dulcinea del Toboso is the most beautiful lady in the world
before they can pass. Should they refuse, they must ready themselves to face him
in battle! These merchants take him a lot less seriously than everyone else has
so far.

Hey man, we'll totally do that as soon as you show us some pictures of this
Dulcinea. We don't want to do a perjury, you know. Nay, you knaves, you must
swear sight unseen otherwise it has no value. No, dude, really, pics or it
didn't happen.
